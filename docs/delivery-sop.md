# Delivery SOP: from "yes" to live and paid

The process for one person delivering a website build. Written to be
followed, not admired. If a step feels like ceremony, it's there because
skipping it is how a first client goes wrong. Internal doc, not published.

**The one rule that protects everything else: no build starts before the
deposit clears, and the site doesn't go live before the balance clears.**

---

## Stage 1: The yes call (30 minutes, same week they say yes)

They've agreed in principle. This call turns "yes" into a defined job.

Cover, in order:

1. **What they actually need.** Pages, booking or contact flow, anything
   the mock promised. Write it down as a list of pages and features while
   they're on the call. This list becomes the scope.
2. **What's out of scope, said out loud.** Copywriting from scratch, logo
   design, photography, "can you also fix my emails". Offer them as paid
   extras or point them elsewhere, but name them now.
3. **Price and payment terms.** 50% deposit to start, 50% before launch.
   Say it on the call, don't spring it in an email after.
4. **Timeline.** Realistic for one person with other clients: 2 to 3 weeks
   from deposit AND content received. Both conditions, always said together,
   because content is what actually delays builds.
5. **One revision round, defined.** "You'll see the design first, we adjust
   it once, then I build. After launch you get two weeks of snags for free."

Same day, send the summary email: scope list, price, terms, timeline,
revision policy. End with "reply 'agreed' and I'll send the deposit
invoice". That email plus their reply is your written agreement. For jobs
over about a thousand pounds, attach a simple one-page terms sheet instead
(see "What will bite you" below).

## Stage 2: Collect before you build

Nothing starts until you have:

- [ ] Deposit paid (Stripe link or bank transfer, whatever clears)
- [ ] Content: the actual words for each page, or written agreement that
      you're adapting their existing copy
- [ ] Images: their photos, or agreement to use stock, agreed on the call
- [ ] Brand bits: logo file (or "use what's on the van"), colours if they
      care, anything they insist on
- [ ] Domain access: registrar login, or delegation of DNS, or at minimum
      the name of whoever controls it. Chase this on day one. It's the
      single most common launch blocker and it's never in your control.
- [ ] The practical details: opening hours, service list, service area,
      phone, email, socials, anything legally required for their trade

Send this as one checklist email straight after the deposit lands. One
email, not six. If content hasn't arrived in a week, one nudge, then the
timeline officially pauses ("the 2 to 3 weeks starts when the content
lands, as agreed").

## Stage 3: Scope freeze

Before designing, write the internal version: pages, features, what's
explicitly excluded, in the same doc where you track the client. Takes ten
minutes. When the "while you're at it, could we also..." message arrives,
and it will, the answer is easy and friendly: "Happy to. That's outside
what we scoped, so I'll quote it separately once the site's live."

## Stage 4: Design pass (days 1 to 4)

Homepage design first, on the real domain-to-be or a preview link. Not a
PDF, a clickable page, because that's what sold them in the first place.

Send it with three questions: what's wrong, what's missing, what do you
love. Give them a deadline for feedback ("any changes by Friday and I stay
on schedule"). This is the ONE revision round from Stage 1. Collect all
their changes in one go, apply once.

## Stage 5: Build (days 4 to 12)

- Build the remaining pages to the approved design
- Wire the forms, booking, and any agents they're paying for, and TEST them:
  submit the contact form, book a fake appointment, ring the number
- Mobile pass on a real phone, not just the dev tools
- Basics that get forgotten: page titles, favicon, 404 page, privacy page,
  Google Business profile pointing at the new domain

Send one mid-build progress note ("halfway, on track for the 18th"). Not
because they need it, but because silence is when clients start ringing.

## Stage 6: Review round (days 12 to 14)

Preview link with everything working. Same rules as Stage 4: one
consolidated list of changes, cosmetic and content fixes included, new
features quoted separately. Apply, confirm done.

## Stage 7: Get paid, then launch

1. Send the balance invoice with "site's ready, it goes live when this
   clears"
2. Balance clears
3. Point the DNS, install on the live domain, SSL check
4. Click every link and submit every form ON THE LIVE DOMAIN
5. Submit the sitemap to Google Search Console

Never launch on a promise to pay. A live site is the only leverage you
have, and it works exactly once.

## Stage 8: Handover (within a week of launch)

- One email with: where the site lives, their logins (password manager
  share or one-time link, not plain text), who owns the domain (them, in
  writing), and what to do when something breaks (email you)
- Two weeks of free snag fixes, then changes are billable or covered by a
  monthly plan
- THE PITCH THAT PAYS TWICE: offer the monthly retainer now, while they're
  happiest. Hosting, updates, tweaks, and the AI agents from £X a month.
  A launch client is the easiest retainer sale you'll ever make.
- Three weeks post-launch: ask for the Google review and the referral.
  "Know anyone else whose site undersells them?"

---

## What will bite you (learn it here, not on client one)

- **No written agreement.** The Stage 1 summary email is the minimum. Past
  about a grand, use a one-page terms sheet: scope, price, payment points,
  revision limit, who owns what on full payment, and that unpaid means not
  launched. Run a template through a generator or a solicitor once and
  reuse it forever.
- **Starting without the deposit.** A client who won't pay 50% up front
  was never going to pay 100% at the end.
- **Unlimited revisions by accident.** You never said "unlimited", but you
  never said "one round" either. Say it in Stage 1, in writing.
- **Scope creep by niceness.** Every "quick extra" is fine alone. Six of
  them is an unpaid week. The Stage 3 line ("quoted separately once we're
  live") keeps the relationship warm AND paid.
- **The domain hostage.** Their old web guy owns the domain and isn't
  answering. Chase domain access from day one, and never put THEIR domain
  in YOUR name, because next year that hostage-taker is you.
- **Content limbo.** The number one killer of solo timelines. The pause
  clause in Stage 2 is what stops "waiting on their photos" becoming a
  three-month project you can't invoice.
- **Launching on trust.** Covered above. It works exactly once.

## Per-client checklist

Copy this block into the client's doc and tick as you go.

```
CLIENT: ____________  QUOTED: £______  START: ______

STAGE 1: THE YES
[ ] Yes call done, scope list written during call
[ ] Out-of-scope named out loud
[ ] Summary email sent (scope, price, 50/50 terms, timeline, 1 revision round)
[ ] Client replied "agreed" (or terms sheet signed if over ~£1k)

STAGE 2: COLLECT
[ ] Deposit invoice sent
[ ] DEPOSIT PAID (nothing starts before this)
[ ] Content received (words for every page)
[ ] Images received or stock agreed
[ ] Logo / brand bits received
[ ] Domain access sorted (or owner identified and chasing)
[ ] Practical details: hours, services, phone, email, socials

STAGE 3 + 4: SCOPE + DESIGN
[ ] Internal scope doc written (incl. exclusions)
[ ] Homepage design on preview link
[ ] Feedback deadline given
[ ] Revision round 1 of 1 applied

STAGE 5: BUILD
[ ] All pages built to approved design
[ ] Forms / booking / agents wired AND tested with a real submission
[ ] Mobile pass on a real phone
[ ] Titles, favicon, 404, privacy page
[ ] Mid-build progress note sent

STAGE 6: REVIEW
[ ] Full preview shared
[ ] Consolidated changes applied, confirmed done

STAGE 7: PAID, THEN LIVE
[ ] Balance invoice sent
[ ] BALANCE CLEARED
[ ] DNS pointed, SSL confirmed
[ ] Every link + form tested on the LIVE domain
[ ] Sitemap submitted to Search Console

STAGE 8: HANDOVER
[ ] Handover email: logins, domain ownership in their name, support route
[ ] Snag window explained (2 weeks)
[ ] Monthly retainer offered
[ ] Week 3: review + referral ask
```
