# Cold Outreach Playbook

For reaching local service businesses, 10 to 15 a week. The angle is always
the same: you've already rebuilt a piece of their site, so the email shows
work instead of pitching it. Internal doc, not published.

Ground rules before the templates:

- Build the mock FIRST. Every email below leans on a link or screenshot of
  their own business looking better. Without it you're just another agency
  email, and those get deleted on the subject line.
- One email, one idea, one link. If it doesn't fit on a phone screen without
  scrolling, cut it.
- Send from your own name, not a company inbox. Reply-ability is the whole
  game.
- Send Tuesday to Thursday, morning. Mondays get buried, Fridays get ignored.

---

## Template 1: The straight mock

Best default. Use when their site is visibly dated and you've mocked their
homepage.

**Subject: I rebuilt your homepage (have a look)**

> Hi {first name},
>
> I'm Will, I build websites for {trade}s around Bristol. I had a look at
> {business name}'s site this week and, honestly, it undersells the work.
> Your reviews are excellent and the site doesn't show it.
>
> So I rebuilt your homepage to show you what I mean: {link or screenshot}.
>
> That took me an afternoon and it's yours to look at either way. If you
> like it, I'll walk you through what a full version costs on a 15 minute
> call. If not, no bother, it was good practice.
>
> Will

## Template 2: The missed-call angle

Use for phone-led trades (plumbers, electricians, roofers) where you rang
and nobody picked up, or their contact page is just a number.

**Subject: called you Tuesday, nobody picked up**

> Hi {first name},
>
> Small experiment: I called {business name} on Tuesday around 2pm, like a
> customer would, and it rang out. Fair enough, you were probably up a
> ladder. But that customer's already called the next {trade} on Google.
>
> I build systems that fix exactly that: every missed call gets an instant
> text back, and the website actually books jobs while you're working. I've
> mocked up what your site could look like with that built in: {link}.
>
> Worth 15 minutes? I'll show you the numbers and you can decide from there.
>
> Will, Telos AI (Bristol)

## Template 3: The compliment lead

Use when their work is genuinely good (strong reviews, nice portfolio) but
the web presence is a Facebook page or a 2015 template. Softest of the three.

**Subject: your reviews deserve a better shopfront**

> Hi {first name},
>
> I came across {business name} through {where you actually found them} and
> your reviews are seriously good. {Quote a real review line if you can.}
>
> The thing is, someone finding you online sees {their current site / just a
> Facebook page} first, and it doesn't do that reputation justice. I build
> sites for local service businesses, so I put together a quick version of
> what yours could look like: {link}.
>
> No catch. If it's interesting, I'll talk you through it in 15 minutes.
> If not, keep the mock as a compliment.
>
> Will

## Follow-up: no reply (send 4 to 6 days later)

One follow-up only. Two unanswered emails is an answer.

**Subject: re: {original subject}**

> Hi {first name},
>
> I know these emails land at bad times, so one quick nudge and then I'll
> leave you alone. The mock of {business name}'s site is still here: {link}.
>
> If it's a "not now", that's fine. If it's a "not ever", reply with just
> the word "no" and you won't hear from me again.
>
> Will

## Follow-up: positive reply

Send the same day they reply. The only goal is a booked time, not more
email tennis.

> Hi {first name},
>
> Great. Easiest next step is a quick call so I can hear how you actually
> get work, then I'll tell you exactly what I'd build and what it costs.
> No obligation on either side.
>
> Here's my calendar: {booking link}. Or tell me a time that suits and I'll
> work around you. I'm in Bristol, so if you're local I'm happy to come to
> you instead.
>
> Will

## What to personalise every time

Non-negotiables. If you can't fill these in, don't send it, because they're
the difference between "someone looked at my business" and copy-paste spam.

1. **The mock itself.** Their name, their photos or trade imagery, their
   phone number on it. Thirty seconds of looking should tell them it was
   made for them.
2. **First name of the owner.** It's on Companies House, their Facebook, or
   their reviews. "Hi there" gets deleted.
3. **One true, specific observation.** A real review quoted, the actual day
   and time you called, the thing their current site gets wrong. One is
   enough. Generic flattery reads as a template.
4. **Their trade in your one-liner.** "I build websites for roofers" beats
   "I build websites for businesses" every time you send it.
5. **Where you found them.** Google Maps, a friend's recommendation, their
   van. Real answers only, it's usually the first thing they wonder.

Keep a simple log (spreadsheet is fine): business, owner name, date sent,
template used, mock link, replied or not, follow-up date. Ten a week gets
messy by week three without it.
